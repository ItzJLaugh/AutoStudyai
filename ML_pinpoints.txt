1. Generalization:
  - Train on a diverse dataset with examples from many sources: PowerPoint slides, PDFs, web-based slideshows, lecture notes, and different subjects.
  - Include both “important notes” (definitions, explanations, key points) and “non-content” (navigation, slide numbers, buttons, copyright, etc.).

2. Quality Labels:
  - Carefully label your data as “content” (real notes) or “not-content” (irrelevant UI, instructions, etc.).
  - The more varied and accurate your labels, the better your model will perform for all users.

3. Robust Preprocessing:
  - Clean the text (remove extra whitespace, normalize case, etc.).
  - Consider splitting long text blocks into sentences or bullet points for finer classification.

4. Model Choice:
  - Start with a simple model (e.g., LogisticRegression with TfidfVectorizer).
  - As you grow, consider more advanced models (e.g., transformers like BERT) for better accuracy.

5. Continuous Improvement:
  - Allow users to flag mistakes in extracted notes, and use this feedback to retrain and improve your model.

6. Privacy & Security:
  - Make sure you handle user data securely and transparently, especially if you process notes in the cloud.

Most necessary for your tool:
    
A large, diverse, and well-labeled dataset covering many note-taking scenarios.
A model that prioritizes extracting meaningful, concise, and relevant information, not just text blocks.
Easy ways to update and retrain the model as you get more user data and feedback.
This approach will help your tool work well for a wide range of students and content types, making it truly universal and valuable.
